# Competitive Landscape: Trading & Market Games on Steam
## Research for "Second Chance at a Billion" v2

## 1. STONKS-9800: Stock Market Simulator — the genre's benchmark

**Core loop & UI.** You play a salaryman-turned-trader in 1980s–90s bubble-era Japan. Text-based menus with pixel-art vignettes and CRT shader; stock data is presented as simple price lists and dividend tables rather than deep charting. The loop is buy stocks → collect dividends → expand into real estate, cars, companies, pachinko and horse betting mini-games → manage your health so overwork doesn't hospitalize you. Fictional simulated market, not real data. ([Steam page](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1539140/STONKS9800_Stock_Market_Simulator/))

**Reception.** Overwhelmingly Positive: 97% of ~1,372 English reviews, ~3,070 total. Price £8.50/$9.99, Early Access since July 2023, full release planned 2026. Developer TERNOX (solo Ukrainian dev) publicly confirmed **25,000+ copies sold** by July 2024, validating Gamalytic's estimate within ~10% ([Mezha.Media](https://mezha.media/en/2024/07/08/ukrainian-indie-game-stonks-9800-sold-25-thousand-copies/), [SteamDB](https://steamdb.info/app/1539140/)).

**What players praise.** Vibe above everything. "Amazing detail... the whole game is in theme," soundtrack, animations, "CRT lifestyle" ([Steambase reviews](https://steambase.io/games/stonks-9800-stock-market-simulator/reviews)). Top Steam reviews are emergent-story anecdotes: "Made over a trillion yen, started an illegal company... got killed by yakuza. 10/10" (48.9 hrs); "hella addictive... tickles my brain when I see number go up" (84.3 hrs); "first 20 mins in the game I lost everything, so for the next 3 hours I went fishing" ([Steam reviews](https://steamcommunity.com/app/1539140/reviews/?browsefilter=toprated)). Note how many top reviews are *stories about consequences* (jail, yakuza, employees leaving), not stories about trading.

**What players complain about.** "Doesn't add anything new to the genre," getting "bored fast," crashes that killed multi-month runs, stat-raising that feels punitive (Financial Success drops instantly on any debt), hard-to-read graph colors ([discussions](https://steamcommunity.com/app/1539140/discussions/0/6953050670805106202/)). One 77-hour reviewer's "praise" was a wishlist of missing depth (forex, gambling options).

**Steal / avoid.** Steal: a *committed aesthetic identity* (its single biggest asset), consequence-driven emergent stories players want to retell, side activities that give losers something to do (fishing!). Avoid: its actual trading model is shallow; it wins on theme, not mechanics. Our game currently has neither the aesthetic commitment nor the anecdote-generation of STONKS-9800.

## 2. Insider Trading — the most direct structural competitor

**Core loop & UI.** Released Feb 18, 2026 ($10.99/£10.99 tier, dev Naiive). A Balatro-like roguelike deckbuilder where the deck IS the market: each trading day the game pulls 10 cards that push stock prices up/down in sequence; you buy/sell around your own deck's price movements to hit a weekly money target. Retro CRT terminal interface, cards trigger in sync with the soundtrack. 120+ cards, 60+ perks, unlockable characters, recessions as disruptive events ([Steam page](https://store.steampowered.com/app/3166810/Insider_Trading/)).

**Reception.** Mostly Positive, 79% of 578 reviews (demo was Overwhelmingly Positive; the full release slipped). Got major press purely on concept: PC Gamer ran two features ("[They made Balatro for stock trading...](https://www.pcgamer.com/games/roguelike/they-made-balatro-for-stock-trading-and-i-fear-i-may-never-write-another-article-ever-again/)", "[...the first time I've ever screwed up in a deckbuilder by making my deck too good](https://www.pcgamer.com/games/roguelike/this-balatro-inspired-stock-trading-roguelike-marks-the-first-time-ive-ever-screwed-up-in-a-deckbuilder-by-making-my-deck-too-good/)"), plus [Aftermath](https://aftermath.site/insider-trading-stock-market-balatro-roguelike/) and Adventure Gamers.

**Praise.** The counter-intuitive strategy (you must crash prices to buy low; "too much success prices you out of the market") reads as "a really complicated strategic puzzle." Aftermath's Nicole Carpenter praised the "satisfying retro interface and rhythmic soundtrack."

**Complaints (this is the gold).** From Steam negative reviews: (1) **late-run RNG**: "If you end up with a working deck it isn't because of what you did, but because of the lucky rolls you got. Wins feel unrewarding... losses even more frustrating"; (2) **punishing success is confusing**: winning "too much" guarantees a lost run; (3) forced deck bloat: "you end up with a deck that is at least 50% garbage"; (4) **fantasy mismatch**: "Insider trading is about being filthy rich, but this felt more akin to being a nervous nerd who doesn't want to be caught with his hand in the cookie jar" ([Steam reviews](https://steamcommunity.com/app/3166810/reviews/?browsefilter=toprated)). Even a positive press reviewer admitted "I can't really say that I totally understand what's going on."

**Steal / avoid.** Steal: the roguelike-target framing clearly attracts press and the Balatro audience; sync'd audio/tactility matters. Avoid: opacity. Their core failure is that players can't tell *why* they won or lost. Our real-data hook is actually an antidote: the market isn't RNG, it's history, and skill = remembering/learning what happened. Also note their fantasy-mismatch complaint: players want to FEEL rich and powerful. Our "$10K to $1B, SEC breathing down your neck" fantasy is stronger, and v2 should lean into the power fantasy (lifestyle, spending, swagger), not just meters.

## 3. The Invisible Hand — narrative wrapper, shallow sim

**Core loop & UI.** First-person immersive sim (May 2021, $14.99, Power Struggle Games/Fellow Traveller): you're a broker at FERIOS hitting daily profit targets, trading via an in-world monitor with news feeds and insider tips; you can manipulate markets, lobby, and dodge a financial investigation. ([Steam page](https://store.steampowered.com/app/628200/The_Invisible_Hand/))

**Reception.** Very Positive, 82% of ~500-657 reviews; Metacritic 69; SteamDB-estimated 5.7K–15.7K copies (2022) ([SteamDB](https://steamdb.info/app/628200/), [Steambase](https://steambase.io/games/the-invisible-hand/steam-charts)). Now sells at 80% off, a sign of exhausted full-price demand.

**Praise.** "Extremely engrossing," satisfying satire, "hits all the reward centers of the brain" ([Gaming Cypher](https://gamingcypher.com/the-invisible-hand-review-for-steam/), [PC Gamer](https://www.pcgamer.com/im-making-millions-while-going-morally-bankrupt-in-this-stock-trading-sim/)).

**Complaints.** The most detailed negative Steam review (9.5 hrs) is a masterclass in what kills trading games: "Tweet say stock go up? Buy stock... Tweet say stock go down? Short it" — i.e., **news-to-trade mapping so literal it becomes a "mindless clicker game"**; UI "takes up way too much space"; the game "doesn't follow its own rules"; endgame stock resets feel like "forced grinding." Others: "campaign is stupidly short... 1-2 hours," story ignores whether you actually broke the law ([Steam reviews](https://steamcommunity.com/app/628200/reviews/?browsefilter=toprated), [Cultured Vultures](https://culturedvultures.com/the-invisible-hand-review/)).

**Steal / avoid.** Steal: the investigation/pressure fantasy works; players love the satire of finance culture. Avoid: (a) if news deterministically signals trades, trading collapses into Simon Says — our historical events must require interpretation (magnitude? timing? which stock?); (b) consequences that don't respond to player behavior feel fake — our SEC system already does this better; keep it legible.

## 4. Trade Bots: A Technical Analysis Simulation — the other "real data" game

**Core loop & UI.** By Cinq-Mars Media. Gamified backtesting: you trade candlestick charts of **real historical US stock data**, but tickers are *mystery stocks* revealed only after trading, explicitly to prevent foreknowledge. Unlock TA indicators and bots with in-game capital ([official site](https://www.cinqmarsmedia.com/tradebots/index.html), [Steam reviews](https://steamcommunity.com/app/1899350/reviews/?browsefilter=toprated)).

**Reception.** **Mixed, 63% of ~100 reviews** ([Steam](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1899350/Trade_Bots_A_Technical_Analysis_Simulation/), [SteamSpy](https://steamspy.com/app/1899350)). Complaints: tools locked behind grind, no stop-loss/position-sizing options, "buggy, and the button to submit bug feedback is even broken," progression requires watching embedded YouTube tutorials.

**Lesson.** This proves real historical data alone doesn't sell — it was framed as *homework* (learn TA, hidden tickers, education-first). It deliberately removed the one thing that makes real data fun: **knowing what happens next**. We do the opposite: named stocks, named years, the fantasy of hindsight. That's the correct read of the same raw material.

## 5. Wall Street Raider (2026 Steam remaster) — depth as identity

**Core loop & UI.** The 40-year-old cult sim (since 1986) relaunched in Early Access May 14, 2026 at £22.49 (~$25-30). Text-based, point-and-click; simulates 1,000+ companies, M&A, derivatives, tax, banking; open-source web-tech UI the community can mod ([Steam](https://store.steampowered.com/app/3525620/Wall_Street_Raider/), [official site](https://www.wallstreetraider.com/)).

**Reception.** Very Positive, 94% of 185 reviews. Praised as "the closest simulation of a real life stock market that exists today" and "the 'Dwarf Fortress' of tycoon games." Complaints: "hard learning curve," bugs and lag, "very overwhelming at first" — the dev had to build a wiki ([Outsider Gaming](https://outsidergaming.com/wall-street-raiders-second-act-how-a-cult-finance-sim-is-getting-a-new-life-on-steam/)).

**Steal / avoid.** Its audience tolerates ugly UI *because* depth is the identity and the fantasy is being a corporate raider, not a chart-reader. Avoid competing on simulation depth — we lose. But note: 94% positive at a $25 price shows finance-sim players pay well when the game respects their intelligence.

## 6. Big Ambitions — what scale looks like, and where even winners fail

Very Positive (92% of 5,400+ reviews), ~$6.5M gross revenue, launched with 93K wishlists driven by streamers ([GameDiscoverCo deep dive](https://newsletter.gamediscover.co/p/deep-dive-how-big-ambitions-hit-it), [games-stats](https://games-stats.com/steam/game/big-ambitions/)). It's a first-person business sim, not a trading game, but its complaint profile is the canonical tycoon failure mode: "after around day 60... repeatedly sleeping to earn money... boring despite the first 10 hours being enjoyable"; "running a large business feels similar to running a tiny shop"; no automation/delegation late-game; "the endgame is absolutely lacking" ([Steam discussions](https://steamcommunity.com/app/1331550/discussions/0/6620894968760998392/)). **Lesson for us:** our exponential quarterly targets ($15K→$1B) are the right skeleton, but each order of magnitude must change *how you play* (new instruments, new crimes, new risks), or Q5–Q8 becomes Big Ambitions' day-60 sleep loop with bigger numbers.

## 7. Rags to Riches (2026 Steam remake) — closest to "historical period" framing

The 1993 Interplay classic remade, released March 17, 2026, with "historically accurate scenarios" (the 1929 Crash, the 1980s) ([Steam](https://store.steampowered.com/app/4353020/Rags_to_Riches/), [MobyGames](https://www.mobygames.com/game/15388/rags-to-riches-the-financial-market-simulation/)). But it trades only four abstract assets (Bonds, Chips, Gold, Oil) — historical *flavor*, not historical *data*. Too new/small for meaningful review mining. It confirms appetite for period-piece trading but doesn't touch our mechanic.

## 8. Bitburner — the stock market as a puzzle to be beaten

Free incremental hacking game, 94% of ~5,900 reviews. Its stock market is beloved because it's "not like casino gambling, but like an ocean shore with ebbs and tides that can be mostly predicted mathematically to take advantage of" ([Steam guide](https://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=2860330999), [Steam](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1812820/Bitburner/)). **Lesson:** players adore markets that are *learnable systems* rewarding mastery. Our historical data is exactly that — veterans should be able to say "it's March 2000, get OUT of tech" and feel like geniuses. Surface the learnability.

## 9. Luck be a Landlord — framing and juice lessons

94% of 5,300+ reviews, ~$9.99 ([Steam](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1404850/Luck_be_a_Landlord/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luck_Be_a_Landlord)). Rock Paper Shotgun: "a compulsive celebration of silliness, and a successful parody" of capitalism; Pocket Gamer: "simple on the surface but full of depth." Lessons: (1) a hostile system to beat ("pay rent or die" = our quarterly targets/SEC) gives money-earning emotional stakes; (2) visible symbol synergies (cats drink milk) = our unlock shop should produce *combos players screenshot*; (3) anti-capitalist satire framing generates press goodwill in this niche.

## 10. Smaller / adjacent

- **Stock Exchange Game** — "based on thousands of world events," i.e., closest to our news system, but **Mixed 53% of 13 reviews** at £4.29: real events without craft or identity sells nothing ([Steam](https://store.steampowered.com/app/3177130/Stock_Exchange_Game/)).
- **TradingLife** — Early Access sim using **real historical 1-minute bars** (crypto/gold/FX), positioned as trader training, not fantasy ([Steam](https://store.steampowered.com/app/4359150/TradingLife/)).
- **Liquidated – The Crypto Roguelike** — upcoming: crypto trading + rent pressure + a psychological stress system that distorts audio/visuals ([Steam](https://store.steampowered.com/app/4190090/Liquidated__The_Crypto_Roguelike/)). Watch it; the stress-feedback idea is stealable for SEC heat.
- **Crypto Is Dead** — delisted; **Crypto Miner Tycoon Simulator** — 83% of 306 reviews, players cite tedium ([Steam](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1859290/Crypto_Miner_Tycoon_Simulator/)).
- **Free web "market timing" games** ([engaging-data](https://engaging-data.com/market-timing-game/), [Personal Finance Club](https://personalfinanceclub.com/time-the-market-game/)) use real S&P data to prove you *can't* time the market — evidence the fantasy resonates broadly, but these are 5-minute toys, not games.

## Synthesis

### What the market rewards in this niche
1. **A committed aesthetic identity beats simulation fidelity.** STONKS-9800 (97%, 25K+ sales) is a shallow sim wrapped in a perfect 1980s-Japan CRT vibe. Insider Trading got two PC Gamer features off a retro terminal + music-synced cards. Generic-looking trading games (Stock Exchange Game, Trade Bots) die at Mixed. Our painted-NYSE art direction is the right instinct; v2 must push it into every screen and sound.
2. **Anecdote generation.** The top reviews of every successful game here are stories ("got killed by yakuza 10/10"). Runs must end in retellable ways. Our arrest/fired/billionaire endings are raw material — make each ending screen a shareable story artifact.
3. **Learnable systems + power fantasy.** Bitburner's market is loved because mastery pays. Insider Trading is dinged because players "can't tell what's going on" and feel like "a nervous nerd" instead of a rich criminal. Legibility + swagger.
4. **Satire sells.** Luck be a Landlord, The Invisible Hand, and STONKS-9800 all wear anti-capitalist or absurdist humor; press loves the angle.

### Common failure modes (all observed in real reviews)
- **"Number go up" with no decisions** — Invisible Hand's "tweet says up, buy" clicker collapse.
- **Late-game sameness** — Big Ambitions' day-60 wall; scale must change verbs, not just digits.
- **Opaque outcomes / RNG blame** — Insider Trading's "wins feel unrewarding" problem.
- **Education framing** — Trade Bots turned real data into homework; Mixed 63%.
- **Punishing players for succeeding** without clear telegraphing (Insider Trading, STONKS' debt-stat).
- **Run-destroying bugs** in long runs (STONKS crashes) — fatal in a 728-day-run game like ours; autosave/resume integrity is a retention feature.

### The gap we can own — and the uniqueness claim is verified, with caveats
Searched specifically for games combining real historical market data with foreknowledge. **No Steam game does what we do.** The only real-data games are Trade Bots (tickers deliberately *hidden* to prevent foreknowledge), TradingLife (trader-training tool, crypto/FX minute bars), and free web market-timing toys; Rags to Riches has historical *scenarios* but four generic assets. Nobody offers "you know Apple moons in 2004, Lehman dies in September 2008 — profit from it, without the SEC noticing." That fantasy is unclaimed, it is instantly pitchable in one sentence (press bit hard on Insider Trading's much weaker one-liner), and it solves the genre's core failure mode: our market is neither RNG nor deterministic Simon-says — it's *history*, and player skill is knowledge. The caveat: the hook only lands if the game constantly *reminds* you it's real (real dates, real headlines, "on this day" flavor, achievements for nailing famous events like shorting the GFC). If the data just looks like any squiggly line, we've built Trade Bots. The v2 priority order this research suggests: (1) make historicity visible and celebrated everywhere, (2) STONKS-level aesthetic commitment, (3) legible SEC/risk cause-and-effect, (4) each quarter tier changes the verbs, (5) run endings engineered for anecdotes.
