# Roguelike Retention Research: "One More Run" Design for Second Chance at a Billion

## 1. The Established Theory: What Actually Drives "One More Run"

The literature and postmortems converge on six drivers, and it's worth being blunt about which ones your game currently has (marked ✓/✗):

**Short, low-cost restarts (✗ at 2-year runs).** Analyses of roguelike addiction consistently cite run length as the foundation: 10-30 minute runs "lower the mental cost of starting over" so the brain treats another attempt as cheap ([retrostylegames.com](https://retrostylegames.com/blog/why-are-roguelike-games-so-engaging/), [Medium: The Psychology of 'Just One More Run'](https://medium.com/@haleymckenzie710/the-psychology-of-just-one-more-run-why-roguelikes-are-addictive-07332dc66b07)). A 728-tick run, even at 10x speed, is over a minute of pure waiting per game-quarter if the player has no decisions to make. Vampire Survivors caps runs at 30 minutes precisely so failure invites immediate retry.

**Failure that feels productive (partial ✓).** Hades' explicit design philosophy was that "players should never feel like they just wasted their whole run" ([Lords of Gaming: Desiring Death](https://lordsofgaming.net/2021/08/desiring-death-how-hades-changes-the-roguelike-formula/)). Your pity PP, Golden Parachute, and Dead Man's Switch bonuses are exactly this pattern. Good instinct, keep it.

**Near-miss losses (✗ mostly).** Vampire Survivors' developer came from the gambling industry and deliberately engineered the near-miss effect: "every run in which players don't reach the 30 minute mark will elicit this feeling" ([University of Portsmouth analysis](https://www.port.ac.uk/news-events-and-blogs/blogs/popular-culture/vampire-survivors-how-developers-used-gambling-psychology-to-create-a-bafta-winning-game)). Getting fired for missing Q3 by $30K should be the emotional core of your retention loop, and the run-end screen should rub it in: "You were 94% of the way to Q3. One more trade would have done it."

**Variety of starting conditions and builds (✗ — this is your core problem).** Procedural generation "forces players to adapt their strategies rather than executing predetermined optimal paths" ([Glitchwave on Luck Be a Landlord](https://glitchwave.com/game/luck-be-a-landlord/)). Slay the Spire generates variety through character × card offer randomness; FTL through events and ship unlocks ([FTL GDC postmortem](https://gdcvault.com/play/1018034/Designing-Without-a-Pitch-FTL)). Your randomness currently lives only in the player's imperfect memory of history, which decays to zero with experience. This is the section 5 problem.

**Mastery curve (partial ✓).** The Grid Sage Games / Roguelike Radio discussion frames the deepest retention as the player getting better, not the character ([gridsagegames.com](https://www.gridsagegames.com/blog/2025/08/designing-for-mastery-in-roguelikes-w-roguelike-radio/)). There IS real mastery in your game (position sizing, SEC heat management, drawdown timing) but it's masked because "buy the winners you remember" dominates.

**Meta-progression drip (✓ but miscalibrated, see section 3).**

## 2. Decision Density: Interesting Choices Per Minute

Sid Meier's GDC 2012 framework is the canonical source: games are "a series of interesting decisions," and a decision is interesting when it has **tradeoffs** (meaningful cost vs. benefit), is **situational** (interacts with current game state, no fixed right answer), allows **personal expression** (playstyle), and is **persistent** (visible consequences), with enough information that the player isn't guessing blind ([Game Developer: GDC 2012 Sid Meier](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/gdc-2012-sid-meier-on-how-to-see-games-as-sets-of-interesting-decisions), [GDC Vault: Interesting Decisions](https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1015756/Interesting)). His two archetypal decision shapes, risk-vs-reward and short-term-vs-long-term, map perfectly onto trading, which is why this game concept is fundamentally sound.

How the greats manufacture density:

- **Slay the Spire** forces a choice roughly every 30 seconds: pick 1 of 3 cards (or skip!), pick a path node, play a hand under energy constraints. Mega Crit used a metrics server tracking every player decision to ensure no choice was dominant ([Game Developer: Slay the Spire metrics](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/how-i-slay-the-spire-i-s-devs-use-data-to-balance-their-roguelike-deck-builder), [GDC Vault](https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1025731/-Slay-the-Spire-Metrics)). The "skip the card reward" option is famously the mark of an interesting choice: adding a good card can still be wrong for your build.
- **Luck Be a Landlord** offers one small choice per spin (add 1 of 3 symbols) but makes every choice a synergy question: "a flower is modest alone, but with bees both multiply" ([games.gg analysis](https://games.gg/luck-be-a-landlord/)). Interest comes from combinatorics, not choice size.
- **FTL** uses a pressure clock (the rebel fleet) so "wait and farm" is never free; every jump is a gamble on an unknown event ([FTL postmortem](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/designing-without-a-pitch---an-em-ftl-em-postmortem)).
- **Vampire Survivors** "barely lets you go a few seconds without something good happening" ([kokutech design analysis](https://www.kokutech.com/blog/gamedev/design-patterns/power-fantasy/vampire-survivors)).

**Diagnosis for your game:** the dominant player action between quarterly deadlines is *wait at 10x speed*. That is zero decision density. You have a pressure clock (quarterly targets) but no per-minute choice cadence. The fix isn't more buttons; it's a steady drip of expiring, mutually exclusive opportunities: weekly tip drafts (pick 1 of 3, like a card reward), events that demand a response before they pass, margin calls, board mandates. Every mechanism in section 5 is chosen partly for the decisions-per-minute it adds.

## 3. Meta-Progression: Done Right vs. Grind-Gate

Four reference points span the spectrum:

- **Hades (Mirror of Night)** is widely held up as the best-in-class: upgrades are customizable, respec-able, and each rank is small enough that skill still dominates; its purpose is emotional ("no run is wasted"), not power-gating ([TheGamer: Mirror of Night does upgrades right](https://www.thegamer.com/hades-mirror-of-night-roguelite-progression/)).
- **Rogue Legacy** is the community's canonical *negative* example: incremental stat purchases mean the early game is deliberately gimped and you pay a "farm tax to access the real game" ([ResetEra meta-progression thread](https://www.resetera.com/threads/do-you-like-meta-progression-in-your-roguelikes-roguelites.1341955/)). The general principle: upgrades must "enhance the experience without trivializing the run-to-run skill challenge" ([bugnet.io: How to Design a Roguelite Meta-Progression](https://bugnet.io/blog/how-to-design-a-roguelite-meta-progression)).
- **Vampire Survivors** gets away with raw power upgrades because runs are short and gold income is high; every run pays out visibly ([natrowley.com](https://www.natrowley.com/the-addictive-nature-of-vampire-survivors/)).
- **Balatro** is the purist's proof: essentially *no power meta-progression at all*. Unlocks are sideways content: new Jokers, new decks (different rules, not stronger), Stakes (harder difficulty), and 20 challenge runs ([Rogueliker Balatro interview](https://rogueliker.com/balatro-interview/), [Balatro Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balatro)). Progression is knowledge and challenge access, never stats.

**Opinionated diagnosis of your shop:** your unlocks split into two categories with opposite value. *Sideways unlocks* (new sectors, illegal actions, tools, career paths) are Balatro-style: they add options and decision surface. Keep and expand these. *Power unlocks* (leverage tiers, fee reduction, SEC-decay buffs) are Rogue Legacy-style stat taxes: they make the already-degenerate strategy ("buy known winners, hold") strictly stronger and make early runs feel artificially weak. Worse, in a game where the winning line is known, power unlocks shorten the time-to-solved. I'd rebalance the shop so at least 70% of PP spend buys *new kinds of decisions* (new archetypes, new event decks, new crime types, new run modifiers) rather than bigger numbers. Also copy Hades: allow a free respec, and copy Vampire Survivors: make PP income generous enough that a new player buys something after nearly every run.

## 4. Difficulty Structures: Longevity Per Unit of Dev Effort

Ranked by (longevity gained) / (dev cost), based on what these systems cost their developers:

1. **Ascension ladder (StS)** — 20 levels, each a one-line rule change ("elites are stronger," "start damaged"). Trivial to implement, and it converted StS from a 20-hour game into a 500-hour game because each level re-poses every decision ([Eneba StS review](https://www.eneba.com/hub/games/slay-the-spire-review/)). *For you:* SEC aggressiveness, tighter targets, higher fees, lower arrest threshold, "the SEC remembers you between quarters." Almost pure config.js changes.
2. **Player-selected modifiers coupled to rewards (Hades Pact of Punishment / Balatro Stakes)** — the genius detail is that players *choose which* pain to take and get proportional reward (Heat thresholds gate rare resources) ([RPG Site: Pact of Punishment](https://www.rpgsite.net/feature/10287-hades-pact-of-punishment-heat-modifiers-and-how-to-maximize-your-rewards), [Wikipedia: Hades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hades_(video_game))). Self-selected difficulty also lets weaker players stay comfortable, which matters given your refund problem. *For you:* a "Board Demands" screen before each run: toggle modifiers (no shorting, 2x fees, aggressive SEC, mystery year) for a PP multiplier.
3. **Daily seeded run (Spelunky)** — one attempt, same seed for everyone, leaderboard. Gamasutra called it "understated genius": it removes randomness as an excuse, creates water-cooler comparison, and spawned video-sharing communities ([Game Developer: the understated genius of the Spelunky Daily Challenge](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/the-understated-genius-of-the-i-spelunky-i-daily-challenge)). StS's Daily Climb layers modifiers on top ([StS wiki](https://slay-the-spire.fandom.com/wiki/Daily_Challenge)). Medium cost for you (Steam leaderboards API), but it's the single strongest daily-retention tool, and your fixed real data is actually an *advantage* here: "Today's run: start July 2007, no tech stocks, arrest threshold 65" is trivially reproducible.
4. **Authored challenge runs (Balatro's 20 challenge decks)** — hand-designed scenario decks. Higher authoring cost per hour of play, but great for marketing beats ("Can you survive 2008 with shorts disabled?").

## 5. Application: Manufacturing Run Variety When History Is Fixed

The strategic insight first: **since prices can't be random, randomize everything around the prices — the player's information, constraints, goals, and pressure.** Poker uses a fixed 52-card deck; variety comes from hidden information and position. Your 733 tickers × 24 years is a far bigger deck. The enemy isn't fixed data, it's *perfect information plus no constraints*. Every mechanism below attacks one of those two.

**A. Hidden Year / Mystery Start (deduce the date from news).** Start the run at an undisclosed date; the news feed gives period clues (no explicit years), and the player must infer "is this 2006 or 2014?" before committing capital. Their historical knowledge transforms from a cheat sheet into a *deduction minigame*, and misidentification is a spectacular failure story ("I thought it was 2019... it was 2007"). This is NetHack's unidentified-potion pattern applied to macro. **Decision created:** how much to bet before you're sure; which cheap "probe" trades reveal the era. **Dev cost: Low-Medium** (you already have the news event data; scrub years from it). **Proof:** identification mechanics in classic roguelikes; Balatro/StS prove players love inference under uncertainty. This should be a difficulty modifier and the default for daily runs.

**B. Randomized tip quality (information as the run's random resource).** This is my top recommendation. Weekly/monthly, the player drafts 1 of 3 "sources" (a whistleblower, a chatroom, an astrologer, a senator's spouse) whose tips have hidden accuracy (e.g., 90% / 60% / lies) discovered only through use, with better sources carrying SEC heat or cost. Now the run's "build" is your *information network*, and it's different every run even though prices never change. **Decision created:** trust calibration, push-your-luck on hot sources, SEC heat vs. edge tradeoff — Meier's risk-vs-reward in pure form, on a steady drip cadence that fixes decision density. **Dev cost: Medium** (tips are just "ticker X moves Y% by date Z," verifiable against data you already have). **Proof:** Luck Be a Landlord's random symbol offers ([games.gg](https://games.gg/luck-be-a-landlord/)); StS card rewards.

**C. Archetype starts (characters).** Day Trader (fast cooldowns, tiny capital, position limits), Hedge Fund (starts with $1M but investors demand quarterly redemptions), Crypto Bro (only speculative assets, insane volatility mods), Activist Short-Seller (profits only from shorts, SEC loves you), Politician's Nephew (free insider tips, permanent SEC floor). Each invalidates the "buy Apple and wait" line differently. **Decision created:** run-level identity that changes which trades are even legal/viable. **Dev cost: Medium** (mostly config: starting cash, mods, allowed actions — your TRADING_MODES scaffolding already points this way). **Proof:** StS's four characters are its single biggest variety lever; FTL's ships likewise ([FTL postmortem](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/designing-without-a-pitch---an-em-ftl-em-postmortem)).

**D. Board mandates / rotating constraints per quarter.** Each quarter, the board imposes a random directive: "no tech this quarter," "deliver 15% using shorts only," "hold at least 5 sectors," with bonus PP for compliance. Cheap procedural variety that forces the player off their memorized line *every 91 days*. **Decision created:** portfolio construction under constraint; comply vs. eat the penalty. **Dev cost: Low** (a constraints table + a check in quarterly.js). **Proof:** Against the Storm's orders/forest mysteries, which keep a stable core loop fresh by "poking the player out of the balanced state" ([Game Developer on Against the Storm](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/how-against-the-storm-managed-to-mix-city-building-and-roguelite-play)).

**E. SEC ascension + randomized arrest threshold leaning harder.** You already randomize the arrest threshold (good — that's your near-miss engine). Make an explicit 20-level ladder: SEC decay slows, thresholds tighten, investigations persist across quarters, illegal-action heat scales, eventually legal-but-suspicious profits draw heat. At high ascension the game becomes about *laundering your edge*, which is a genuinely novel fantasy. **Decision created:** how much visible profit can you afford. **Dev cost: Low** (config multipliers). **Proof:** StS Ascension, Hades Heat ([RPG Site](https://www.rpgsite.net/feature/10287-hades-pact-of-punishment-heat-modifiers-and-how-to-maximize-your-rewards)).

**F. Daily run.** Fixed start date + modifier bundle + hidden year + one attempt + Steam leaderboard. **Dev cost: Medium.** **Proof:** Spelunky ([Game Developer](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/the-understated-genius-of-the-i-spelunky-i-daily-challenge)). Highest daily-retention payoff of anything on this list.

**G. Alt-history divergence events (use sparingly).** Random mid-run counterfactuals announced via news: "In this timeline, the Fed doesn't cut rates," applying a modeled shock to a sector. **Decision created:** your memorized future just became unreliable — hedge or double down? **Dev cost: High** (needs believable price perturbation; risks violating your "real data" USP and your no-made-up-data rule unless clearly flagged as timeline divergence). I'd rank this last: modifiers A/B/D achieve the same doubt for a tenth of the cost. **Proof:** closest analogue is Crusader Kings-style alt-history flavor; no roguelike proves it, which is itself a warning.

**H. Random starting date within the year (not Jan 1).** Trivial variance amplifier: starting April 2008 vs. September 2008 is a different game. **Dev cost: Trivial.** Do this immediately as part of daily/mystery modes.

**Priority order if I were you:** E + H (config-only, ship in a week) → D (quarterly mandates, fixes mid-run dead air) → B (tip drafting, fixes decision density and is the real "build system") → C (archetypes) → F (daily runs) → A (hidden year as a marquee modifier) → G (probably never). And in parallel, rebalance the PP shop away from raw power toward unlocking the above content, per section 3 — that converts your existing meta-progression from a Rogue Legacy grind into a Balatro content ladder.
